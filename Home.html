    Double-Entry Bookkeeping

    Double-entry bookkeeping is a system where every transaction is recorded in at least two accounts: a debit and a credit.

    Double-Entry Bookkeeping

    Double-entry bookkeeping ensures that the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) remains in balance after each transaction.

    Debits and Credits

    In double-entry bookkeeping, debits and credits must always equal each other, ensuring the accounting equation stays balanced.

    Debits and Credits

    Debits represent increases in assets and expenses or decreases in liabilities and revenues, while credits represent decreases in assets and expenses or increases in liabilities and revenues.

    Assets

    Assets are resources owned by the business, such as cash, inventory, equipment, and property.

    Assets

    Debits increase assets, while credits decrease assets.

    Liabilities

    Liabilities are obligations owed by the business, such as loans, accounts payable, and accrued expenses.

    Liabilities

    Credits increase liabilities, while debits decrease liabilities.

    Equity

    Equity represents the owner's stake in the business and is calculated as assets minus liabilities.

    Equity

    Owner's investments and retained earnings increase equity (credits), while owner withdrawals decrease equity (debits).

    Revenue

    Revenue is the income earned by the business from its primary activities, such as sales of goods or services.

    Revenue

    Revenue increases equity (credits).

    Expenses

    Expenses are the costs incurred by the business to generate revenue, such as salaries, rent, and utilities.

    Expenses

    Expenses decrease equity (debits).

    Chart of Accounts

    A chart of accounts is a list of all accounts used by a business, organized by category and assigned a unique account number.

    Chart of Accounts

    The chart of accounts provides a systematic way to record and track financial transactions.

    Trial Balance

    A trial balance is a report that lists the balances of all accounts in the ledger to ensure debits equal credits.

    Trial Balance

    If the trial balance does not balance, it indicates errors in the accounting records that need to be corrected.

    General Ledger

    The general ledger is a complete record of all financial transactions of a business, organized by account.

    General Ledger

    The general ledger serves as the foundation for preparing financial statements and analyzing the financial health of the business.
